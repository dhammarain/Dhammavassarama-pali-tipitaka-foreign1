道元 [py] Dao4yuan2 [wg] Tao-yüan [ko] Towôn [ja] ドウガン Dougen ||| Dougen; (1200-1253) prominent Japanese Zen master, founder of the Soutou 曹洞 sect of Japanese Buddhism. After learning with Kouin 公胤 of Miidera 三井寺 for a period of time, he next moved on to Kenninji 建仁寺 where he had a chance to study with Esai 榮西, the founder of the Japanese Rinzai school. Following Esai's death, he traveled to Song China together with Esai's successor Myouzen 明全. Here he was exposed to a number of the influential streams of Ch'an, including that of Dahui 大慧, but his major awakening came as a result of his encounter with the Caotong 曹洞 master Rujing 如淨 at Tiantong shan 天童山. In 1227 Dougen returned to Japan where he began to institute the Caotong (Soutou) teaching at Kenninji and then moving to Fukakusa where he opened up Koushouji 興聖寺. Here he spent about ten productive years during which he composed his most important work, the Shoubougenzou 正法眼藏. His final important period followed this, when he moved into the mountains of Echizen prefecture to establish a center for pure meditational practice away from the secular influences of the capital. This new monastery was originally named Daibutsuji 大佛寺 and was later named Eiheiji 永平寺, which survives to the present as an active monastery for the practice of Zen. In addition to the Shoubougenzou (95 fasc.) he composed numerous influential works, including the Fukanzazengi 普勸坐禪(1 fasc.), the Eiheishingi 永平清規 (2 fasc.), and Gakudou youshin shuu 學道用心集 (1 fasc.)
